# Anger is the enemy of wisdom

![bird in the sky](/blog/img/bird-in-the-sky.jpg)

Like anyone else, I am susceptible to emotional fluctuations. Anger arises from time to time, though I wish it did not. How can we break free from this emotional turbulence?

This week, a chaotic incident at work stirred me up. The emotion burst out before I could even register what was happening. In that moment, it was like a volcano erupting — lava splattering into the sky, flowing downhill through the forest, destroying every living thing in its path. By the time the eruption subsided, only lifeless silence remained, burying all my regrets beneath it.

I repented, deeply ashamed of my failure to control my temper. After that day, I began tracing back to the root of my anger. It was not the incident itself, but my expectation that things should have gone differently — that people should have acted the way I wanted. This expectation clashed with reality, and anger was the explosion of that collision.

Anger is indeed the enemy of wisdom. When rage takes over, reason steps out. We say things we do not mean, make decisions we later regret, and damage relationships that take years to rebuild. In that state, we are no wiser than a child throwing a tantrum.

I have since adopted a simple practice: when I feel the heat rising, I pause. I take a deep breath and count to ten. I remind myself that this moment of anger will pass, but the words I say will not. This small pause gives my rational mind a chance to catch up.

It is not easy. Some days I still fail, and the volcano erupts again. But each time, the interval between trigger and reaction grows a little longer, and the damage grows a little smaller. That is progress enough.

Anger may be the enemy of wisdom, but patience is its ally. And patience, like any muscle, grows stronger with practice.
