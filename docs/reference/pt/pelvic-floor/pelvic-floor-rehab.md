# 骨盆底復健 **Pelvic Floor Rehabilitation**

::: warning 警告
This document is for educational and clinical reference purposes only. Individualized assessment and treatment by a qualified pelvic health physiotherapist is essential before initiating any pelvic floor rehabilitation program.
:::

---

## Overview 概述

Pelvic floor dysfunction (PFD) encompasses a spectrum of conditions affecting the muscles, connective tissues, and nerves of the pelvic floor. These disorders significantly impact quality of life, yet remain under-reported and under-treated due to stigma and lack of awareness.

### Common Conditions 常見病症

| Condition                         | Chinese            | Prevalence                                | Key Features                                                     |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------ | ----------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Stress Urinary Incontinence (SUI) | 壓力性尿失禁       | 25–45% of women                           | Leakage with coughing, sneezing, lifting, or exercise            |
| Urge Urinary Incontinence (UUI)   | 急迫性尿失禁       | 15–30% of women                           | Sudden strong urge followed by involuntary leakage               |
| Overactive Bladder (OAB)          | 膀胱過動症         | 12–17% of adults                          | Urgency with or without urge incontinence, frequency, nocturia   |
| Pelvic Organ Prolapse (POP)       | 器官脫垂           | 50% of parous women (symptomatic in ~20%) | Descent of bladder, uterus, rectum, or vaginal vault             |
| Chronic Pelvic Pain               | 慢性骨盆腔疼痛     | 5–26% of women                            | Persistent pain in the pelvis lasting >6 months                  |
| Postpartum Dysfunction            | 產後骨盆底功能障礙 | Up to 50% postpartum                      | Weakness, incontinence, organ descent following vaginal delivery |

::: warning 警告
Pelvic floor dysfunction is not exclusively a female condition. Men following prostatectomy, individuals with chronic constipation, and those with neuromuscular disorders are also at significant risk.
:::

## Anatomy 骨盆底解剖

### Pelvic Floor Muscles 骨盆底肌肉

The pelvic floor is a complex, multi-layered structure forming the inferior boundary of the pelvic cavity.

| Muscle            | Chinese    | Origin                     | Insertion          | Function                                                                     |
| ----------------- | ---------- | -------------------------- | ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Pubococcygeus** | 耻骨尾骨肌 | Pubic symphysis            | Coccyx             | Main component of levator ani; supports pelvic organs                        |
| **Puborectalis**  | 耻骨直腸肌 | Pubic symphysis            | Anorectal junction | Maintains anorectal angle; essential for continence                          |
| **Iliococcygeus** | 髂尾骨肌   | Arcus tendineus            | Coccyx             | Provides horizontal support to pelvic floor                                  |
| **Coccygeus**     | 尾骨肌     | Ischial spine              | Sacrum/coccyx      | Supports sacrum; closes posterior pelvic outlet                              |
| **Perineal body** | 會陰體     | Central tendon of perineum | —                  | Anchor point for bulbospongiosus and superficial transverse perineal muscles |

### Levator Ani Complex 提肛肌群

The **levator ani** (提肛肌) is the primary muscle group of the pelvic floor, comprising three distinct parts:

- **Pubococcygeus** — the largest and most superficial component; plays a critical role in continence and sexual function
- **Puborectalis** — forms a U-shaped sling around the anorectal junction; essential for fecal continence
- **Iliococcygeus** — the most posterior and thinnest portion; provides structural support

### Supportive Fascia and Connective Tissue 支持性筋膜與結締組織

| Structure                             | Role                                                         |
| ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Endopelvic fascia                     | Connective tissue envelope supporting pelvic organs          |
| Arcus tendineus fasciae pelvis (ATFP) | Thickened band of fascia running from pubis to ischial spine |
| Pubocervical fascia                   | Supports bladder and anterior vaginal wall                   |
| Rectovaginal fascia                   | Supports rectum and posterior vaginal wall                   |
| Urogenital diaphragm                  | Provides additional support to urethral and vaginal openings |

### Role in Continence and Organ Support 排尿控制與器官支撐功能

The pelvic floor serves four primary functions:

1. **Support** — Maintains position of pelvic organs against gravity and intra-abdominal pressure
2. **Continence** — Voluntary closure of urethral and anal sphincters during increased abdominal pressure
3. **Sexual function** — Controls vaginal and anal sphincter tone; contributes to arousal and orgasm
4. **Postural stability** — Works synergistically with the deep trunk muscles for lumbopelvic stability

::: warning 警告
Pelvic floor dysfunction often coexists with deep core dysfunction. A comprehensive approach addressing the entire lumbopelvic cylinder (diaphragm, transversus abdominis, multifidus, pelvic floor) is essential for optimal outcomes.
:::

## Assessment 評估

### Clinical Assessment 臨床評估

A thorough pelvic floor assessment includes subjective history, external and internal examination, functional testing, and validated outcome measures.

### Oxford Grading Scale 牛津肌力評估量表

| Grade | Description    | Chinese  | Clinical Significance                             |
| ----- | -------------- | -------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| 0     | No contraction | 無收縮   | Complete absence of palpable contraction          |
| 1     | Flicker        | 微弱顫動 | Barely perceptible contraction                    |
| 2     | Weak           | 弱       | Complete contraction without hold                 |
| 3     | Moderate       | 中等     | Complete contraction with hold against resistance |
| 4     | Good           | 良好     | Complete contraction with moderate resistance     |
| 5     | Strong         | 強       | Complete contraction against strong resistance    |

### PERFECT Scheme

The PERFECT scheme provides a structured evaluation of pelvic floor muscle function:

| Component             | Description                                                 | Measurement               |
| --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **P**ower             | Maximum voluntary contraction strength                      | Oxford grade (0–5)        |
| **E**ndurance         | Longest hold at maximum contraction                         | Seconds                   |
| **R**epetitions       | Number of repetitions of maximum holds                      | Count                     |
| **F**orce             | Number of contractions reaching grade 4+                    | Count (out of total reps) |
| **E**very contraction | Consistency of contraction quality                          | Consistency rating        |
| **C**oordinate        | Ability to activate correctly without accessory recruitment | Yes/No/Partial            |
| **T**iming            | Coordinated activation with cough or exertion               | Yes/No/Partial            |

### POP-Q System (Pelvic Organ Prolapse Quantification)

| Point | Description                                    | Normal Value                 |
| ----- | ---------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Aa    | Anterior vaginal wall, 3 cm proximal to hymen  | −3 to +3 cm                  |
| Ba    | Most distal point of anterior vaginal wall     | −3 to +3 cm                  |
| C     | Cervical stump or vaginal cuff                 | Varies with uterine position |
| D     | Posterior fornix / posterior vaginal wall      | Varies                       |
| Ap    | Posterior vaginal wall, 3 cm proximal to hymen | −3 to +3 cm                  |
| Bp    | Most distal point of posterior vaginal wall    | −3 to +3 cm                  |
| Gh    | Genital hiatus                                 | Measured in cm               |
| Pb    | Perineal body                                  | Measured in cm               |
| TVL   | Total vaginal length                           | Measured in cm               |

### Pad Test 尿墊試驗

| Duration | Procedure                                                                                    | Positive Result  |
| -------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------- |
| 1-hour   | Weigh pad before and after standardized activities (cough, stair climbing, walking, running) | Weight gain ≥ 2g |
| 24-hour  | Weigh all pads used over 24 hours during normal daily activities                             | Weight gain ≥ 4g |

## Pelvic Floor Muscle Training (PFMT) 骨盆底肌肉訓練

### Proper Technique 正確技巧

#### Verbal Biofeedback 口語生物回饋

The physiotherapist provides real-time verbal cues to guide correct activation:

- "Imagine drawing your pelvic floor muscles up and inward — like lifting a blueberry inside the vagina"
- "Feel the muscles around the urethra and anus drawing upward"
- "Avoid bearing down — the sensation should be a gentle lift, not a push"
- "Notice if your abdominals, gluteals, or adductors are compensating — try to isolate the pelvic floor"

#### Digital Palpation 數位觸診

Internal assessment via digital palpation allows:

- Confirmation of correct muscle identification
- Assessment of contraction strength (Oxford scale)
- Evaluation of endurance and repetition capacity
- Detection of paradoxical (bearing down) activation
- Assessment of pain or tenderness

::: warning 警告
Digital palpation should only be performed by trained pelvic health professionals with appropriate consent and in a safe, comfortable environment.
:::

### Exercise Technique Progression

| Level                  | Description                              | Sets × Reps     |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | --------------- |
| Isometric hold         | Sustained contraction at moderate effort | 5 sec × 10 × 3  |
| Progressive hold       | Increasing hold duration                 | 10 sec × 10 × 3 |
| Quick flicks           | Rapid contraction–relaxation cycles      | 1 sec × 15 × 3  |
| Functional integration | Activating with cough, sit-to-stand      | Contextual sets |

## Phase 1: Awareness (Weeks 1–2) 第一階段：覺察期

**Goal:** Establish correct pelvic floor identification and activation without accessory muscle recruitment.

### Objectives

- Accurate identification of pelvic floor muscles
- Achieve isolated activation without co-contraction of abdominals, gluteals, or adductors
- Establish breathing coordination (inhale to relax, exhale to activate)
- Baseline assessment using Oxford scale and PERFECT scheme

### Exercises

| Exercise                                               | Description                                                                                                              | Frequency                |
| ------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------ |
| Pelvic floor scan                                      | Instruct patient to "lift" and "release" the pelvic floor, holding each contraction for 2 seconds                        | 10 reps × 3 sets, 3×/day |
| Diaphragmatic breathing with pelvic floor coordination | Inhale through nose, allowing pelvic floor to gently descend; exhale through mouth, allowing pelvic floor to gently lift | 5 minutes, 3×/day        |
| Supine hook lying activation                           | Lying with knees bent, feet flat; focus on pelvic floor contraction without abdominal bracing                            | 10 reps × 3 sets, 2×/day |

::: warning 警告
If the patient reports pain during internal examination or internal exercises, STOP. Reassess for hypertonic pelvic floor dysfunction. Kegel exercises are contraindicated in hypertonic conditions.
:::

### Cues for Correct Activation

| Cue Type       | Verbal Cue                                                              |
| -------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Imagery        | "Imagine picking up a small marble with your pelvic floor"              |
| Tactile        | "Place your finger at the vaginal opening and feel for inward pressure" |
| Mirror         | Use of perineal mirror for visual confirmation of elevation             |
| Proprioceptive | "Feel the muscles around the urethra and anus draw inward"              |

---

## Phase 2: Strengthening (Weeks 2–6) 第二階段：強化期

**Goal:** Increase pelvic floor muscle strength, endurance, and coordination.

### Objectives

- Achieve grade 4+ on Oxford scale
- Increase endurance hold to 10 seconds
- Complete 10 repetitions with consistent quality
- Integrate quick flick contractions for reflex continence

### Progressive Protocol

| Week | Hold Duration | Reps × Sets            | Frequency | Notes                                 |
| ---- | ------------- | ---------------------- | --------- | ------------------------------------- |
| 2–3  | 5 sec         | 10 × 3                 | 3×/day    | Monitor for accessory recruitment     |
| 3–4  | 8 sec         | 10 × 3                 | 3×/day    | Add quick flicks                      |
| 4–5  | 10 sec        | 10 × 3                 | 3×/day    | Introduce pre-activation before cough |
| 5–6  | 10 sec        | 10 × 3 + 15 flicks × 3 | 3×/day    | Functional integration exercises      |

### Quick Flick Training 快速收縮訓練

| Parameter         | Specification                                                              |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Contraction speed | Rapid maximal contraction                                                  |
| Hold time         | 1 second                                                                   |
| Relaxation        | Complete relaxation between reps                                           |
| Sets              | 3 sets of 15 reps                                                          |
| Purpose           | Trains fast-twitch fibers for reflex continence (e.g., sneezing, coughing) |

### Functional Integration 功能性整合

Begin integrating pelvic floor activation into daily activities:

- **Sit-to-stand:** Activate pelvic floor before rising from a chair
- **Cough preparation:** Pre-contract before voluntary cough
- **Lifting:** Coordinate pelvic floor with transversus abdominis when lifting objects
- **Stair climbing:** Activate on each step ascent

---

## Phase 3: Functional Integration (Weeks 6–12) 第三階段：功能性整合

**Goal:** Maintain pelvic floor function under conditions of increased intra-abdominal pressure and in sport-specific contexts.

### Objectives

- Sustain pelvic floor activation during compound movements
- Achieve automatic pelvic floor coordination with exertion
- Return to sport or high-demand activities with continence confidence

### Progressive Loading Protocol

| Exercise             | Description                                                                                 | Progression                         |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| **Bodyweight squat** | Perform with pre-activation of pelvic floor; maintain engagement through descent and ascent | Add dumbbells, kettlebells, barbell |
| **Deadlift**         | Hip hinge with pelvic floor co-contraction; exhale on lift                                  | Progressive loading                 |
| **Cough challenge**  | Voluntary cough with pre-contracted pelvic floor; assess for leakage                        | Increase intensity progressively    |
| **Jumping**          | Double-leg jumps with pelvic floor engagement; progress to single-leg                       | Increase height and repetition      |
| **Running**          | Begin with walk–jog intervals; monitor for incontinence                                     | Progress duration and speed         |

### Sport-Specific Training

| Sport         | Challenge                                        | Training Focus                                  |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Running       | Repetitive impact, high intra-abdominal pressure | Quick flick reflexes, endurance under impact    |
| Weightlifting | Extreme intra-abdominal pressure                 | Maximum pre-contraction, breathing coordination |
| Gymnastics    | Variable loading, high impact                    | Multi-planar coordination                       |
| Tennis        | Rotational forces, explosive movements           | Dynamic pelvic floor stabilization              |
| Swimming      | Reduced gravitational load, breath-holding       | Breath-hold with pelvic floor activation        |

::: warning 警告
Do not progress to high-load or high-impact activities if the patient has grade 3 or below on the Oxford scale or reports ongoing incontinence during basic functional tasks.
:::

## Adjunctive Interventions 輔助治療

### Electrical Stimulation 電刺激

| Type                                        | Parameters                                     | Indication                               |
| ------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) | 10–50 Hz, pulse width 200–400 μs, 15–20 min    | Weak pelvic floor muscles (Oxford 0–2)   |
| Interferential current (IFC)                | 4000 Hz carrier, 1–100 Hz difference frequency | Pain, muscle re-education                |
| TENS for pelvic pain                        | Low-frequency (2–10 Hz), 30–60 min             | Chronic pelvic pain, urgency suppression |

### Biofeedback 生物回饋

| Modality               | Description                                                                            | Evidence                                            |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Manometric biofeedback | Pressure-sensitive balloon measures intravaginal or intra-rectal pressure              | Moderate evidence for improving contraction quality |
| Surface EMG            | Perineal or anal surface electrodes measure pelvic floor and abdominal muscle activity | Useful for identifying accessory muscle recruitment |
| Ultrasound biofeedback | Real-time imaging of pelvic floor movement                                             | Emerging evidence; excellent for patient education  |

### Vaginal Weights 陰道重物訓練

| Weight          | Progression                   | Protocol                                       |
| --------------- | ----------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Start at 20–30g | Progress in 10–15g increments | Insert for 1–2 minutes during upright activity |
| Maximum weight  | 70–80g                        | Progressive loading over weeks to months       |

::: warning 警告
Vaginal weights are contraindicated in the presence of prolapse > Stage II, active infection, or pelvic pain.
:::

### Lifestyle Modification 生活型態調整

| Factor                      | Recommendation                                                        | Evidence Level |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------- |
| **Fluid management**        | 6–8 cups (1500–2000 mL) per day; avoid excessive fluid intake         | Strong         |
| **Weight loss**             | BMI >25: even 5–10% weight loss can significantly reduce SUI          | Strong         |
| **Caffeine reduction**      | Limit to ≤200 mg/day; caffeine is a bladder irritant                  | Moderate       |
| **Smoking cessation**       | Smoking increases cough-related incontinence and collagen degradation | Moderate       |
| **Constipation management** | High fiber (25–30g/day), adequate fluid, avoid straining              | Strong         |
| **Bladder training**        | Scheduled voiding with gradual time increases for UUI/OAB             | Strong         |

## Precautions and Contraindications 注意事項與禁忌症

### Overactive (Hypertonic) Pelvic Floor 過度活躍（高張力）骨盆底

::: warning 警告
**Do NOT perform Kegel exercises in patients with suspected hypertonic (overactive) pelvic floor dysfunction.** Strengthening an already overactive pelvic floor will worsen symptoms.
:::

| Sign of Hypertonic PFD         | Description                                                   |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Pain with internal examination | Discomfort or guarding during digital palpation               |
| Difficulty initiating void     | Hesitancy, straining, or incomplete emptying                  |
| Constipation                   | Pelvic floor unable to relax for defecation                   |
| Dyspareunia                    | Pain during or after intercourse                              |
| Paradoxical bearing down       | Patient pushes out instead of lifting during attempted Kegels |

**Management approach for hypertonic dysfunction:**

1. Internal and external myofascial release
2. Down-training with diaphragmatic breathing
3. Trigger point release
4. Descending perineal exercises (focus on relaxation, not contraction)
5. Bladder retraining and behavioral strategies

### Red Flags 紅旗徵兆

| Finding                               | Action                                          |
| ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| New-onset incontinence in men         | Rule out neurological cause; refer to urologist |
| Blood in urine                        | Refer for medical evaluation immediately        |
| Saddle anesthesia                     | Rule out cauda equina syndrome                  |
| Progressive neurological deficit      | Urgent neurological referral                    |
| Inability to void / urinary retention | Refer for urodynamic evaluation                 |

## Special Populations 特殊族群

### Postpartum 產後復健

| Phase              | Timing       | Focus                                                     |
| ------------------ | ------------ | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| Acute postpartum   | 0–6 weeks    | Gentle pelvic floor awareness, breathing, wound healing   |
| Subacute           | 6–12 weeks   | Progressive strengthening, light functional integration   |
| Return to exercise | 3–6 months   | Gradual return to running, jumping, and impact activities |
| Long-term          | 6–12+ months | Sport-specific training, ongoing maintenance              |

::: warning 警告
All postpartum individuals should undergo pelvic floor assessment before returning to high-impact exercise, regardless of delivery mode (vaginal or cesarean).
:::

**Postpartum-specific considerations:**

- Episiotomy or perineal tearing may affect muscle integrity
- Diastasis recti coexists frequently; address the entire lumbopelvic system
- Breastfeeding hormones affect tissue laxity; progress exercises accordingly
- Sleep deprivation affects motor learning; keep sessions short and focused
- Mental health screening is essential — pelvic floor dysfunction is linked to postpartum depression

### Post-Prostatectomy 前列腺切除術後

| Timing            | Focus                                      | Expected Progression                                  |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Pre-surgery       | Baseline assessment, PFMT education        | Establish correct technique before surgery            |
| 0–6 weeks post-op | Gentle PFMT, catheter management, mobility | Grade 1–2 on Oxford scale                             |
| 6–12 weeks        | Progressive strengthening                  | Grade 2–3                                             |
| 3–6 months        | Functional integration, return to activity | Grade 3–4                                             |
| 6–12 months       | Sport-specific, maintenance                | Continence recovery typically achieved by 6–12 months |

::: warning 警告
Post-prostatectomy incontinence is primarily due to sphincter weakness. Early PFMT is strongly associated with faster continence recovery.
:::

### Pelvic Organ Prolapse 盆腔器官脫垂

| POP Stage     | PFMT Approach                                                     |
| ------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Stage I–II    | PFMT is first-line conservative treatment; aim to reduce symptoms |
| Stage III–IV  | PFMT may reduce symptoms; surgical consultation warranted         |
| Post-surgical | PFMT to support surgical outcomes and prevent recurrence          |

**Key principles for prolapse management:**

- Teach the "knack" — pre-contract pelvic floor before coughing or lifting
- Encourage diaphragmatic breathing to reduce chronic intra-abdominal pressure
- Avoid high-impact activities if symptomatic
- Pessary fitting may complement physiotherapy in advanced cases
- Emphasize long-term maintenance — prolapse is a chronic condition

## Evidence Base 循證依據

### Cochrane Reviews

| Review                                                                       | Key Findings                                                                                                            |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cochrane: PFMT for urinary incontinence in women** (Dumoulin et al., 2023) | PFMT is effective for stress, urge, and mixed incontinence; superior to no treatment and behavioral interventions alone |
| **Cochrane: PFMT for urinary incontinence in men** (Anderson et al., 2015)   | PFMT accelerates continence recovery after prostatectomy; moderate-quality evidence                                     |
| **Cochrane: Conservative treatment for POP** (Hagen & Stark, 2013)           | PFMT may improve prolapse symptoms; evidence limited by study quality                                                   |

### Clinical Practice Guidelines

| Organization                                                    | Recommendation                                                                            |
| --------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **APTA** (American Physical Therapy Association)                | PFMT is a first-line treatment for SUI; pelvic floor assessment by trained PT recommended |
| **AUGS** (American Urogynecologic Society)                      | Conservative management (including PFMT) should be offered before surgical intervention   |
| **NICE** (UK National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) | Offer supervised PFMT as first-line treatment for SUI and prolapse                        |
| **ICS** (International Continence Society)                      | PFMT should be individualized; supervised training improves outcomes                      |

### Key Evidence Summary

- Supervised PFMT is **more effective** than unsupervised PFMT
- Minimum **3 months** of consistent training is required for measurable strength gains
- **Combination therapy** (PFMT + biofeedback or electrical stimulation) may provide additional benefit for low-grade weakness
- PFMT has **no significant adverse effects** when performed correctly
- **Maintenance exercise** is essential — gains are lost with cessation of training
