Systematic Approach to Dermatologic Differential Diagnosis
Step 1: Identify the PRIMARY LESION ( morphology — fundamental rule )
Primary Lesion	Definition
Macule	Flat, ≤1 cm, color change only
Patch	Flat, >1 cm
Papule	Raised, ≤0.5 cm, solid
Plaque	Raised, >0.5 cm, flat-topped
Nodule	Raised, 0.5–2 cm, deep/dermal
Tumor	>2 cm
Vesicle	Fluid-filled, ≤0.5 cm
Bulla	Fluid-filled, >0.5 cm
Pustule	Pus-filled
Wheal	Transient, edematous, itchy
Scale	Flaky keratin layer
Crust	Dried exudate (serum/blood/pus)
Erosion	Partial epidermal loss, heals without scar
Ulcer	Full epidermal ± dermal loss, scars
Fissure	Linear crack
Lichenification	Thickened skin with accentuated markings
---
Step 2: Assess DISTRIBUTION & CONFIGURATION
Pattern	What it tells you
Photo-distributed (sun-exposed areas)	Photodermatitis, lupus, drug eruption
Flexural (elbow/knee creases)	Atopic dermatitis
Extensor (elbows/knees)	Psoriasis
Seborrheic (scalp, face, central chest)	Seborrheic dermatitis
Dermatomal (follows nerve)	Herpes zoster
Intertriginous (skin folds)	Candidiasis, erythrasma, inverse psoriasis
Acral (hands/feet)	Dyshidrotic eczema, pompholyx, hand-foot-and-mouth
Generalized / erythrodermic	Drug reaction, psoriasis, cutaneous T-cell lymphoma
Annular (ring-shaped)	Tinea corporis, granuloma annulare, erythema chronicum migrans
Target-shaped	Erythema multiforme
Linear	Contact dermatitis (poison ivy), lichen striatus
Koebner phenomenon (lesions in trauma areas)	Psoriasis, lichen planus, vitiligo
---
Step 3: Categorize by the 4 MAJOR GROUPS
Group A — INFLAMMATORY / ECZEMATOUS (itch is dominant)
Disease	Key clues
Atopic dermatitis	Flexural, early onset, personal/family atopy
Contact dermatitis	Exposure history, geometric/linear pattern
Seborrheic dermatitis	Scalp, eyebrows, nasolabial, greasy scale
Nummular eczema	Coin-shaped plaques, extremities
Stasis dermatitis	Lower legs, venous insufficiency, edema
Dyshidrotic eczema	Sides of fingers/toes, vesicles
Lichen simplex chronicus	Localized lichenification from scratching
Group B — PAPULOSQUAMOUS (papules + scale, less itch than Group A)
Disease	Key clues
Psoriasis	Extensor, silvery scale, nail pitting, Auspitz sign
Pityriasis rosea	Herald patch, Christmas tree pattern on trunk
Lichen planus	Purple polygonal planar papules, Wickham striae, oral lesions
Pityriasis versicolor	Hypopigmented/fawn macules on trunk, fine scale
Secondary syphilis	Palms/soles copper macules, lymphadenopathy
Drug eruption	Temporal link to new medication, morbilliform
Cutaneous T-cell lymphoma	Chronic, pruritic patches/plaques, often misdiagnosed as eczema
Group C — VESICULOBULLOUS (blisters)
Disease	Key clues
Herpes simplex	Grouped vesicles on erythematous base, painful
Herpes zoster	Dermatomal, prodromal pain
Varicella (chickenpox)	"Dewdrop on rose petal," centrifugal
Pemphigus vulgaris	Flaccid bullae, positive Nikolsky sign, mucosal involvement
Bullous pemphigoid	Tense bullae on flexural areas, elderly, negative Nikolsky
Erythema multiforme	Target lesions, mucous membrane involvement
Stevens-Johnson syndrome / TEN	Widespread blistering, mucosal erosions, drug-induced emergency
Dermatitis herpetiformis	Intensely pruritic vesicles on elbows/knees/buttocks, gluten-sensitive
Pompholyx	Deep-seated vesicles on palms/soles
Group D — INFECTIONS / INFESTATIONS
Disease	Key clues
Impetigo	Honey-colored crust, contagious, children
Cellulitis	Warm, erythematous, tender, systemic signs
Erysipelas	Well-demarcated, raised border, face/lower leg
Folliculitis	Pustules at hair follicles
Furuncle / Carbuncle	Deep follicular abscess
Tinea (dermatophytosis)	Annular, scaly, active border, central clearing, KOH+
Candidiasis	Bright red, satellite pustules, intertriginous
Scabies	Burrows, interdigital, nocturnal itch, contact history
Pediculosis	Lice/nits in hair, associated excoriation
Molluscum contagiosum	Umbilicated papules, children
Warts (HPV)	Verrucous papules, disrupted dermatoglyphics
Leprosy (Hansen's)	Anesthetic patches, thickened nerves
Group E — VASCULAR / PURPURIC
Disease	Key clues
Urticaria	Wheals, evanescent, <24h
Vasculitis (leukocytoclastic)	Palpable purpura, lower legs
Henoch-Schönlein purpura (IgA vasculitis)	Palpable purpura, abdominal pain, arthritis, nephritis
Schamberg's disease (pigmented purpura)	Non-palpable, cayenne pepper spots
Petechiae / Ecchymoses	Platelet vs coagulation disorders
Chilblains (pernio)	Red/purple digits, cold exposure
Group F — PIGMENTARY / TUMOR / MISCELLANEOUS
Disease	Key clues
Vitiligo	Well-demarcated depigmentation, symmetrical
Melasma	Facial brown patches, hormonal/photosensitive
Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation	History of previous inflammation
Seborrheic keratosis	Stuck-on waxy papules, elderly
Actinic keratosis	Rough, scaly, sun-damaged skin, premalignant
Basal cell carcinoma	Pearly border, telangiectasias, rodent ulcer
Squamous cell carcinoma	Keratotic, ulcerated, hard, sun-exposed
Malignant melanoma	ABCDE: Asymmetry, Border irregular, Color variegated, Diameter >6mm, Evolving
Dermatofibroma	Dimple sign when pinched
Neurofibromatosis	Café-au-lait spots, neurofibromas, axillary freckling
Tuberous sclerosis	Ash-leaf spots, angiofibromas, shagreen patch

## Step 4: Use the MNEMONIC — **ABCDEFG** for on-the-spot DDx
- **A** — Atopic / Allergic / Autoimmune
- **B** — Bacterial / Bullous
- **C** — Contact / Candidal / Cutaneous T-cell
- **D** — Drug / Dermatophyte / Dermal tumor
- **E** — Eczema / Erythema multiforme / Endocrine
- **F** — Fungal / Follicular / Factitial
- **G** — Granulomatous / Genetic / Gonococcal

Step 5: Narrow by Time Course (Acute vs Chronic)
Acute (hours–days)	Likely causes
Urticaria	Allergic, drug, infection
Contact dermatitis	Irritant/allergen exposure
Drug eruption	Recent medication
Viral exanthem	Fever, systemic symptoms
Herpes zoster	Prodromal pain → vesicles
Chronic (weeks–years)	Likely causes
Psoriasis	Stable plaques
Atopic dermatitis	Relapsing-remitting
Cutaneous T-cell lymphoma	Progressive, refractory
Lupus erythematosus	Photosensitivity, systemic features
Acne vulgaris	Chronic course
---
## Step 6: Confirm with DIAGNOSTIC TOOLS
1. **Dermoscopy** — distinguish benign vs malignant pigmented lesions
2. **KOH prep** — fungal hyphae (dermatophytes), pseudohyphae (Candida), spores (Pityriasis versicolor)
3. **Wood's lamp** — coral red (erythrasma), yellow-green (tinea capitis/T. tonsurans), blue-white (vitiligo)
4. **Skin biopsy** — gold standard for uncertain cases (H&E, special stains, immunofluorescence)
5. **Patch test** — allergic contact dermatitis
6. **CBC / CRP / ANA** — systemic involvement
7. **Tzanck smear** — multinucleated giant cells (HSV/VZV)

Quick Summary Algorithm
1. What is the primary lesion? → narrows to differential group
2. Where is it? → distribution pattern
3. How long? → acute vs chronic
4. Symptoms? → itch (inflammatory) vs pain (infection/ischemia/vascular) vs asymptomatic (tumor/pigmentary)
5. Associated systemic signs? → fever, arthralgia, mucosal involvement
6. Biopsy or KOH → confirm
